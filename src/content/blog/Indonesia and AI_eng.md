# Indonesia and AI: We Use It But Don't Understand What We're Using

> **Personal opinion** | Technology & Society

---

Indonesia records 1.4 billion visits to AI services per year, third in the world, behind only the United States and India.[^1] Sounds impressive, right? But there's one question that rarely gets asked: *what are people actually using AI for, and do they understand how it works?*

Honestly? The answer is a little unsettling.

---

## The Core Problem: AI Treated as a God, Not a Tool

This is the most fundamental issue, and the one that worries me most.

Most Indonesians who use AI, especially students and young workers, aren't using it as a *tool* to accelerate work they already understand. They're using it as a *replacement* for understanding itself. As if AI is an all-knowing oracle: just throw in a prompt, copy the output, done.

Students submit AI-generated work without grasping the material. Freelancers have AI write proposals without knowing the client's context. Content creators generate articles without reading a single source.

**And this isn't just an impression. The data backs it up.**

A 2025 MIT Media Lab study with 54 university students found some pretty alarming results:[^2]

- Students who exclusively used ChatGPT to write essays showed the **lowest brain activity** compared to those who used search engines or wrote on their own
- **83% of the AI group couldn't recall key points** from essays they had just "written"
- Brain connectivity (alpha and theta waves) was nearly **cut in half**
- They wrote 60% faster, but their relevant *cognitive load* dropped by 32%

What's worse: the effects didn't reverse after they stopped using AI. Brain activity remained lower even after the study ended.

Researchers call this **"cognitive debt"**, an accumulated cost every time we outsource our thinking to AI.

> *"When participants reproduce suggestions without evaluating their accuracy or relevance, they not only forfeit ownership of the ideas but also risk internalising shallow or biased perspectives."*
> MIT Media Lab, 2025

Yes, this is a global problem. But in Indonesia, there's an extra layer: we don't have a strong safety net for catching AI mistakes. If GPT hallucinates something about Indonesian tax regulations, who notices? If AI generates invalid statistics, who has the expertise to verify them?

**AI is only as useful as the user's capacity to evaluate its output.** Without foundational knowledge in the domain you're working in, AI isn't a multiplier, it becomes a dangerous substitute.

---

## The Indonesian Data Paradox

Here's where it gets ironic.

On paper, Indonesia looks like it's crushing it with AI:

| Metric | Number | Source |
|---|---|---|
| Knowledge workers using GenAI | **92%** (vs 75% global average) | Microsoft & LinkedIn, 2024 |
| People excited about AI in daily life | **41%** of respondents (ranked 4th globally) | Statista Consumer Insights, 2024 |
| Annual visits to AI services | **1.4 billion** (ranked 3rd globally) | GoTo Group, 2024 |
| AI adoption growth in 2024 | **+47%** | Industry Report, 2025 |
| Projected AI contribution to GDP by 2030 | **USD 366 billion/year** | McKinsey |

But what doesn't show up in that table: Indonesia **doesn't appear in the top 50 countries** for AI research output, AI patents, or AI investment, according to the Stanford AI Index.[^3]

So we're one of the world's top three AI user nations, but we contribute almost nothing to developing the technology itself. We're **AI consumers**, not builders.

And that's not necessarily a problem in itself, every country has to start somewhere. The real issue is *how* we're consuming it.

---

## Five Other Problems Making Things Worse

### 1. Extremely Low AI Model Literacy

Most Indonesians know AI by brand, not by concept. It's like how "toothpaste" in Indonesia is universally called "Pepsodent", AI here equals "ChatGPT." Meanwhile, Claude, Gemini, Mistral, LLaMA, Grok, and dozens of other models exist, each with different strengths and weaknesses.

The result? Tool selection is based on familiarity, not fit. A lot of use cases that would be better served by a different model end up being run on the wrong one.

### 2. A Flood of AI Slop Drowning Out Quality Content

This is a global problem, but it hits harder in Indonesia. Informative AI-generated content exists, sure, but it's buried under waves of slop and brainrot. Estimated view ratios between slop and substantive content can reach 4–5:1. Algorithms bear most of the blame, but the audience choosing to engage with it is us.

Plenty of FOMO creators are mass-producing AI content just to farm AdSense, with no thought for the impact on public information quality. The result is an increasingly noisy information ecosystem where finding something worthwhile gets harder every day.

### 3. Subscription Sharing That Defeats the Whole Point

Indonesia is well-known for creative "structured piracy", including reselling AI premium accounts shared among multiple people. The issue isn't just ToS violations. Personal AI subscriptions have context windows and usage limits optimized for one user. Sharing with ten people means nobody gets the full benefit. And the price is still set globally, so what feels like a smart workaround just sacrifices the quality of the experience without saving much.

This is rooted in a real economic problem, average Indonesian incomes genuinely don't line up with global subscription prices. But sharing accounts doesn't solve it, because the outcome is basically the same: no real value extracted.

### 4. AI Influencers Selling Hype Instead of Education

The pattern is too familiar at this point: clickbait thumbnails ("This FREE AI tool is INSANE!!"), ragebait short-form content, and then a CTA to a paid course, exclusive membership, or premium PDF guide. The tools they recommend are often already paid, outdated, or irrelevant to Indonesian use cases.

Indonesia needs creators who genuinely educate on AI, not marketing funnels wearing an educational costume.

### 5. Structural Factors That Can't Be Ignored

Indonesia isn't a country focused on AI development as an industry. Demand for digitally skilled professionals could reach **12 million people by 2030**, and we don't have an ecosystem ready to fill that gap.[^4] Add to that broadband penetration still below 20% in some regions, and the urban-rural access divide remains very real.

---

## Final Thought: A Gap That Could Become Permanent

If these patterns continue, Indonesia's lag in *meaningful* AI adoption will become more and more structural. Not because we're not using AI, we clearly are. But because we're using it in ways that don't build any real capacity.

The countries that will win in the AI era aren't the ones using ChatGPT the most. They're the ones that successfully integrate AI with strong human thinking, using AI to *amplify* understanding, not replace it.

Our stats look great on paper. But if 83% of Indonesian AI users can't remember what they just "worked on" with AI, it's worth asking: *are we actually moving forward, or just looking like we are?*
